Shamik Dasgupta's should-ness

07 Jul, 2025

Philosopher Shamik Dasgupta says we only get “true” facts and “right” actions if the world contains a built-in kind of should-ness that tells us which rules really count. If we stop believing in that hidden should-ness, then our moral codes and truth claims turn out to be nothing more than the habits and goals of our own community. Other people’s codes are on equal ground, no single rulebook wins by nature, and even “Truth” is just a useful label we share.

Far from ending debate, this view simply means our reasons come from us—not from the universe. That is freeing.

  1. The Need for Primitive Normativity [^primitive-normativity] Dasgupta starts with a stark fork. If we want beliefs and actions to be objectively right, we must postulate some basic property that makes one standard matter more than its rivals. He calls this property primitive normativity . One can embrace a metaphysics in which such normativity suffuses reality—normative Neoplatonism [^neoplatonism]—or give it up and accept a Rortyan landscape where objectivity evaporates .

  2. Quietism [^quietism] and the Symmetry Worry Suppose we refuse to ground ethics in metaphysics and instead defend our values “from the inside.” Dasgupta argues that this quietist move cannot stop a normative symmetry [^symmetry] from arising: a rival community guided by “schmeasons” can mirror our moral practice, and nothing inside quietism lets us say our “reasons” trump theirs . Attempts to break the tie just re-describe it; the symmetry persists .

  3. Undoing the Truth Fetish The same structure haunts epistemology. Dasgupta calls our devotion to capital-T Truth a “fetish.” Using a Lewis-style reference magnetism [^magnetism] argument, he shows that any property doing the job of truth will have a doppelgänger, quuth, that does the job just as well. Only primitive normativity could declare one the Real standard, so rejecting it pushes us to pragmatism: correctness is whatever our long-run practice endorses .

  4. From Elitism to Practical Nihilism [^practical-nihilism] In action, elitism [^elitism] says there exists a uniquely significant standard (maximize pleasure, obey God, whatever) that rightly guides choice. Elitism entails primitive normativity, so abandoning the latter yields practical nihilism: every decision rule is on a par. The leap from “X is good by this rule” to “Do X” is groundless—just habit or custom . One can still utter true normative sentences, but they carry no extra authority .

  5. Life After Objectivity The quartet of papers sketches a single worldview:

  6. Objectivity in belief or action requires primitive normativity.

  7. Primitive normativity is metaphysically extravagant.
  8. Without it, truth and value collapse into practice-relative standards.
  9. Inquiry and deliberation continue, but their authority is internal—not written into the fabric of reality.

Living with that result is not despair, Dasgupta suggests, but liberation: the world becomes “a blank canvas to paint, not a fixed template to fit” .

  1. An uncomprimising view Dasgupta’s challenge is uncompromising. Keep your faith in objective fact and rightness only if you are ready to buy an other-worldly normative layer. Otherwise, accept that our talk of truth and shoulds is just one more human craft, answerable to nothing beyond itself.

  2. What it means for AI alignment If Dasgupta is right that the universe contains no built-in “ought-making” stuff—no primitive normativity—then AI alignment can’t aim at a single, objectively correct moral target. Instead, an AI can only be “aligned” relative to a practice or community, because that is the only place normative authority lives. This has four big implications:

  3. No final utility function. The dream of coding one master value function that captures “true” morality is a category error; any such function merely encodes somebody’s standards, not nature’s.

  4. Pluralist alignment. Different groups will have rival but equally legitimate rule-books (“reasons” vs “schmeasons”). Safe AI must therefore support procedural mechanisms—negotiation, democratic oversight, federated value modules—rather than hard-wiring one code and calling it universal.
  5. Guardrails over goals. Because objective rightness is off the table, minimal negative constraints (e.g., don’t coerce, don’t maim) look more defensible than ambitious positive directives. Dasgupta’s symmetry argument hints that a shared “floor” of prohibitions is easier to justify than any thick conception of the good.
  6. Ongoing revision, not end-state. Alignment becomes a continual social practice: we steer models the way we steer each other—by updating norms, contesting bad outcomes, and renegotiating standards as our practices evolve. The metric for success is “fit to current communal usage,” not convergence on a timeless moral truth.

[^primitive-normativity]: A basic “ought-making” property that is not reducible to ordinary facts and that singles out one standard as the correct one.

[^neoplatonism]: The view that the whole descriptive world emanates from a fundamental normative Good.

[^quietism]: The program that defends ethical claims only with ethical reasons, refusing outside metaphysical or semantic backing.

[^symmetry]: A situation in which two rival normative frameworks mirror each other, leaving no fact to privilege one over the other.

[^magnetism]: The idea (from David Lewis) that some property best fits our use of a term, acting as a “reference magnet.”

[^practical-nihilism]: The thesis that no decision rule has objective authority; all choices are, at bottom, on the same footing.

[^elitism]: The contrasting thesis that one particular standard of action has primitive practical significance.
