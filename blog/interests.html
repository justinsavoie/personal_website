Some of my interests

01 Jul, 2025

Wilfrid Sellars said that philosophy is about “how things in the broadest possible sense of the term hang together in the broadest possible sense of the term.” That idea—understanding how things hang together—is the main thread running through much of what I find intellectually interesting, whether we pursue it through traditional philosophy or other means.

More specifically, I’d say my interests fall under three broad umbrellas.

First, I’m interested in philosophy—big, general questions like Sellars’, but also more focused ones in metaethics (e.g., how do values fit into a world of facts?), (meta)metaphysics (e.g., is Rorty right that none of it really matters and we should just read novels?), and AI alignment (e.g., is alignment even possible? And what would it mean if we were pragmatists who don’t accept the correspondence theory of truth?). I’m also drawn to ontological questions—like whether what exists is simply what’s useful to our best theories. As Quine puts it: “Our ontological questions... are questions of what to posit in our conceptual scheme so as to maximize simplicity and explanatory power.” But what happens when people fundamentally disagree on what counts as “simple,” or what makes for “good explanatory power”?

Second, I’m drawn to critical, melancholic, even deeply cynical worldviews. Literature plays a big role here. I mostly read in French, so I think of authors like Virginie Despentes, Michel Houellebecq, Constance Debré, and Cioran (who maybe fits better under philosophy, but whatever). In English, I read less, but J.G. Ballard, Ottessa Moshfegh, Easton Ellis or Bukowski come to mind. In some sense, all of literary fiction explores this. I don’t necessarily agree with all of these worldviews—they’re often provocative, transgressive, and critical; Houellebecq is kind of horrible in many ways (his treatment of women is not great)—but I think these ways of looking at the world really do matter. They help us understand the world more clearly. They reveal the blind spots of optimistic, liberal views (they force you ask, for example: is Rawls just deluded with his overlapping consensus stuff, are Scanlon/Habermas deluded with their 'reasons we can justify to one another'). I don't have answers here, I just think it's interesting. I’d include people like Marcuse here, too: technically a philosopher, but his critique of modern society as fundamentally totalitarian obviously feels closer to literature in tone and function than to current Anglo-American technical philosophy. I suppose much of continental philosophy fits here. Freud’s Civilization and Its Discontents is another good example. Put another way: it’s not necessarily pathological to have a pessimistic or melancholic point of view. Perhaps it actually helps us understand “how things in the broadest possible sense of the term hang together.

Third—and this is probably the most loosely defined umbrella—I like building things, mostly with computers. That includes data analysis and computational social science. I think of models and programs as tools we build to better understand the world. Some computer programs (like games) clearly aren't designed to help us make sense of the world in a direct way, but I still think of them as a kind of formalization, an abstraction that models one aspect of life. This includes the statistical analysis of public opinion and behavioral data (what I’ve been doing professionally for the last ten years), the creation of social engagement web apps—but also simply experimenting with AI in a builder’s mindset: using chatbots to explore ideas, classify open-text responses, or build small games.

Above all this, of course, I love spending time with my family and friends. I play sports—mostly ice and ball hockey—and I enjoy art, especially visual art and literature in a broad sense. But when it comes to the things that draw me in intellectually, those three umbrellas are what I keep coming back to.
